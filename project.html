This page acts as my project page for the PP434: Automated Data Visualisation for Policymaking course, part of the MPP programme at LSE. Aims: The aim of this project is to analyze discrepancies between the claimed SBM data (door-to-door collection, segregation, and waste generation) and the Management Information System (MIS) data, and how these discrepancies correlate with the Swachh Survekshan rankings of states.

Part I: Introduction

Overview: Introduction to Swachh Survekshan and Marking Criteria

Swachh Survekshan is an annual urban cleanliness survey under the Swachh Bharat Mission (SBM), launched to encourage cities to improve sanitation and waste management practices. It ranks cities based on their performance, fostering healthy competition among urban local bodies (ULBs).

Evaluation Parameters

The survey uses a comprehensive framework with 9,500 marks, distributed across:

  • Service Level Progress (51%)
    • Marks: 4,830
    • Assesses waste collection, segregation, processing, and sustainable practices.
  • Certification (26%)
    • Marks: 2,500
    • Includes certifications like ODF (Open Defecation Free) and Garbage-Free City ratings.
  • Citizens' Voice (23%)
    • Marks: 2,170
    • Evaluates citizen participation, awareness, and feedback on cleanliness initiatives.

Data: Swachh Bharat Mission (Urban), Swachh Survekshan (Cleanliness Survey) 2023, State Urban Development Departments, Census of India (2011).

Part IV: Policy Response

History shows that modern sewage systems in large cities like London and New York transformed public health by reducing diseases. Public health policy in India should prioritize improving sewage collection and treatment systems in urban and semi-urban areas to meet the goals of becoming an upper-middle-income country in the next decade.

Part V: Conclusion

Improved sanitation reduces child malnutrition and mortality across Indian states. Focused policy interventions can bridge existing gaps and accelerate progress toward better public health outcomes.
